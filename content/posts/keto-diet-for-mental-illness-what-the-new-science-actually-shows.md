If you typed "keto and mental health" into a search engine sometime in early February 2026, you weren't alone. RFK Jr.'s claim on February 4th that a ketogenic diet could "cure" schizophrenia sent search traffic spiking and sparked exactly the kind of debate that makes it hard to know what's actually real. Then, four months later, Scientific American ran a major feature on June 16, 2026 covering keto as a potential treatment for anorexia, schizophrenia, and depression. Suddenly this isn't fringe internet territory anymore. You might be wondering whether there's something genuinely worth paying attention to here, or whether this is another cycle of diet hype dressed up in clinical language. It's a fair question, and the answer is more interesting than either the true believers or the skeptics want to admit.

## The Research Is Real, and It's More Promising Than You'd Expect

Let's start with the study that probably matters most right now. In February 2026, a randomized controlled trial from the University of Oxford, published in *JAMA Psychiatry*, enrolled 88 adults with treatment-resistant depression, meaning people who hadn't responded adequately to at least one antidepressant. After just six weeks, the keto group's PHQ-9 depression scores dropped by 10.5 points compared to 8.3 points in the control group. That's a clinically meaningful gap, not a rounding error. A 10-point drop on the PHQ-9 is the difference between moderate and minimal depression for many people.

Here's what I tell people when they see numbers like that: context matters. This was a short trial, with a relatively small sample. "Treatment-resistant" populations can be notoriously variable. And the control diet wasn't a junk-food free-for-all, so we're not just comparing clean eating to chaos. That said, a well-designed RCT showing that kind of signal in a treatment-resistant population is not nothing. It's actually a significant finding.

Elsewhere, a pilot trial of 23 patients with schizophrenia or bipolar disorder found a 32% reduction in Brief Psychiatric Rating Scale scores and a 31% average improvement in Clinical Global Impression severity after four months on keto. Pilot trials are small and can't prove causation, but a 32% symptom reduction in conditions that are notoriously difficult to treat is the kind of result that justifies larger trials, and those trials are now happening. According to Metabolic Mind, more than a dozen clinical trials of ketogenic therapy for serious mental illness are currently completed or underway.

## What the Anorexia Data Tells Us (and Why It's Surprising)

The finding that caught me most off guard came from UC San Diego School of Medicine, published in June 2026. In their study of participants with anorexia nervosa, 72% scored in the recovered or normal range after following a ketogenic diet. That's a striking number for a condition with historically poor treatment outcomes.

You might be wondering how a high-fat, low-carbohydrate diet could possibly be appropriate for a disorder defined by severe food restriction. It's a legitimate concern, and researchers are asking the same thing. The working hypothesis involves ketosis itself, specifically the possibility that ketones may reduce the obsessive cognitive patterns and anxiety that drive restriction behaviors. The brain runs efficiently on ketones, and there's some evidence that metabolic state influences mood and compulsive thinking. But I want to be honest: the mechanisms here are still being worked out, and anorexia is a life-threatening illness. Nobody should attempt a ketogenic intervention for anorexia outside of close clinical supervision. This is research worth watching, not a home protocol.

## Why "Metabolic Psychiatry" Is Getting Serious Traction

The phrase metabolic psychiatry, which treats mental illness partly as a disorder of brain energy metabolism, has been floating around academic circles for years. What's changed in 2026 is that the research base is starting to catch up to the theory.

The core idea is that the brain is extraordinarily energy-hungry, consuming roughly 20% of the body's total energy despite being only about 2% of its mass. In conditions like schizophrenia, bipolar disorder, and major depression, there's accumulating evidence of impaired glucose metabolism in key brain regions. Ketones offer an alternative fuel source that bypasses some of those glucose-metabolism problems. That's the mechanism researchers are betting on, and it's biologically plausible, which is more than you can say for some diet-disease theories.

What the Oxford trial and the schizophrenia-bipolar pilot both suggest is that this isn't purely theoretical anymore. The signal is showing up in actual patients. That doesn't mean keto belongs in every psychiatrist's toolkit tomorrow. It means the hypothesis deserves rigorous testing, and it's getting it.

## What the RFK Jr. Moment Got Wrong (and Why It Still Matters)

When RFK Jr. said keto could "cure" schizophrenia on February 4, 2026, the backlash from clinicians was immediate and understandable. No responsible reading of the current evidence supports the word "cure." Schizophrenia is a complex, heterogeneous condition. The pilot data is preliminary. Conflating an emerging research finding with a cure claim sets patients up for disappointment and potentially delays or disrupts existing treatment.

But here's the thing: the underlying claim that metabolic interventions might meaningfully reduce symptoms in some patients with serious mental illness is actually supported by early data. The problem wasn't the direction of interest, it was the overreach of the language. Diet claims almost always suffer from this, the leap from "shows promise" to "this fixes it." Responsible researchers in this space are careful not to make that leap, and so should anyone reading about it.

## What This Means If You or Someone You Know Is Struggling

If you're living with depression, bipolar disorder, or another serious mental illness and you're wondering whether keto is worth trying, here's my honest take: it's not a replacement for psychiatric care, medication, or therapy. It may, in the future, become a recognized adjunctive treatment, something you do alongside your existing care. We're not quite there yet in terms of clinical guidelines catching up to the research.

What I'd suggest practically is this: if you're already stable on a treatment plan and curious about metabolic approaches, bring it up with your psychiatrist or care team. Some providers are now familiar with the metabolic psychiatry literature and can guide you thoughtfully. Going it alone with a serious mental illness is a different risk calculation than going keto to lose weight. The stakes are higher, and the need for monitoring is real.

The science here is genuinely exciting. It's also genuinely early. Both things can be true, and keeping them both in mind is what separates a useful framework from wishful thinking.
