Most people pick a keto app the same way they pick a Netflix show: scroll for two minutes, grab something popular, and wonder why it's not working for them three weeks later. I did exactly that when I first started tracking macros, and I spent an embarrassing amount of time logging food in an app that wasn't even designed with low-carb eating in mind.

Here's what I've learned after years of recommending tracking tools to clients: the "best keto app" question has no single answer, and most roundups won't tell you that. The app that keeps a busy nurse on track looks nothing like what works for a retired teacher with time to cook elaborate meals. What I can do is tell you what I actually found when I went deep on this, what the apps get right, where they quietly fail, and which ones are genuinely worth your money.

## What Most Keto Apps Get Wrong From the Start

Carb tracking sounds simple. Net carbs, total carbs, fiber math. But most general nutrition apps (MyFitnessPal being the obvious example) were built around calorie-deficit models, not metabolic ones. The database is massive, which is useful, but the default macro targets will push you toward 50% carbohydrates unless you manually override everything. That's not a keto app. That's a calorie counter wearing a keto costume.

What surprised me was how much this matters psychologically. When the app's progress rings and charts are built around calories rather than macro ratios, people default to thinking in calories. They stop asking "will this kick me out of ketosis?" and start asking "does this fit my calorie budget?" Those are genuinely different questions, and the framing matters.

The better keto-specific apps organize your dashboard around fat, protein, and net carbs as the primary metrics. Calories exist, but they're secondary. That single design choice changes how you think about food.

## The Apps Worth Knowing About (And What I Actually Think)

**Carb Manager** is the one I recommend most often to new clients, and I'm not just saying that because it's popular. It's genuinely well-designed for low-carb eating specifically. The free version covers most of what beginners need: net carb tracking, macro breakdowns, a solid barcode scanner, and meal logging. The premium tier ($40/year or about $8/month if you pay monthly) adds deeper analytics, fasting tracking, blood ketone logging, and a recipe analyzer. Honestly, most people don't need the premium version for the first three months. Try the free tier first.

**Cronometer** is the one I personally use for clients who are doing keto for therapeutic reasons, metabolic health, or who have micronutrient concerns. It's less sexy than Carb Manager but it tracks things the others largely ignore: selenium, choline, B vitamins, the full mineral panel. If you're eating keto long-term and you want to know whether you're actually getting enough magnesium from food (most people aren't), Cronometer will show you. The Gold version is around $45/year. Worth it if you're detail-oriented.

**Keto Diet App** from the same team behind the ketogenic diet book series is more of a guided experience. It includes meal plans, recipes developed specifically for keto, and macro tracking. The interface is clean. The downside: the food database isn't as comprehensive as Carb Manager's, and barcode scanning can be hit or miss with less common products. If you want a more hand-held experience with less flexibility, this one fits.

**Lose It!** comes up a lot in keto communities, and I'll be blunt: it's a decent calorie tracker that you can sort of bend into a keto tracker. It doesn't belong on a keto-specific list. Skip it.

A note on MyFitnessPal specifically: the free version lost offline access and some key features in 2022, and the premium is now $20/month or $80/year. For keto tracking specifically, that price makes no sense when Carb Manager does the job better for less.

## The Features That Actually Matter (And a Few That Don't)

Database size matters more than people admit. You'll eventually scan a product and get zero results, or worse, get three results with different carb counts and no way to know which is accurate. Carb Manager and MyFitnessPal have the largest databases; smaller apps can be frustrating if you eat anything outside mainstream grocery items.

Net carb vs. total carb calculation matters enormously. Some apps calculate net carbs automatically (total carbs minus fiber, and sometimes sugar alcohols). Others show only total carbs and make you do the subtraction yourself. This sounds minor until you're logging 10 foods a day and the math starts to blur.

Recipe builders are underrated. If you cook at home, you need an app that lets you enter ingredients and servings and spits out per-serving macros. Carb Manager and Cronometer both do this well. This feature alone can save you from systematically underestimating what you're eating, which is the most common reason people stall on keto.

Blood glucose and ketone logging: only Cronometer and Carb Manager handle this in any meaningful way. If you're using a continuous glucose monitor or testing ketones with a [Keto-Mojo meter](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=keto+mojo+meter&tag=placeholder), logging those readings alongside food data is genuinely useful. Most apps just ignore this.

What doesn't matter as much as the marketing suggests: social features, step counting integrations, and "AI meal suggestions." These look impressive in screenshots and rarely change how consistently someone tracks.

## The Free vs. Paid Question

I get asked this constantly. My honest answer: start free, pay only when you hit a specific wall.

The free tier of Carb Manager handles basic macro tracking, recipe logging, and barcode scanning. That's 80% of what most people need in the first few months. Upgrade if you want detailed nutrient reporting, unlimited meal plans, or ketone/glucose logging. Don't pay $8-10/month because an app has nicer animations.

If you're going to spend money, pair your app with things that actually improve your results: a decent [kitchen scale](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=food+scale+kitchen&tag=placeholder) (the OXO Good Grips 11-pound scale is around $50 and worth every cent), some quality [electrolyte supplements](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=keto+electrolyte+supplements&tag=placeholder) for the first few weeks, and possibly [MCT oil](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=mct+oil+keto&tag=placeholder) if you're using it therapeutically. ## When No App Is the Right App

I want to say this because most keto content won't: some people do worse with tracking apps. I've had clients with a history of disordered eating for whom obsessive logging made things measurably worse. I've had others who are analytical enough that imperfect data (restaurant meals, guessed portions) created more anxiety than the tracking resolved.

The research on self-monitoring for dietary adherence is generally positive, but it's not universal. If you've tried tracking before and found it stressful rather than clarifying, that's real information about yourself. Some people do better with a simpler rule-based approach (no grains, no sugar, keep carbs under 50g, don't count everything to the gram) than with daily app logging. As always, it's worth talking to a registered dietitian who knows low-carb eating if you're unsure what approach fits your history.

### FAQ

#### Is Carb Manager actually free, or is it free-to-start?

The core features, including macro tracking, barcode scanning, and meal logging, are genuinely free with no time limit. Some premium features (advanced analytics, unlimited recipes, ketone logging) sit behind the paid tier, but you won't hit a paywall on basic daily tracking.

#### Can I use MyFitnessPal for keto?

You can, but you'll need to manually set your macro targets (typically around 70-75% fat, 20-25% protein, 5-10% carbs) and ignore the app's default guidance. The database is large and useful. The keto-specific experience is clunky compared to apps built for it, and the current premium pricing is hard to justify for low-carb users specifically.

#### How accurate are the food databases in these apps?

Honestly, accuracy varies a lot. Barcode-scanned packaged foods are usually reliable. Restaurant items and user-submitted entries can be significantly off. For anything you eat regularly and care about, cross-check with the USDA FoodData Central database, which is free and reasonably authoritative.

#### Do any keto apps support continuous glucose monitor data?

A few apps are building CGM integrations, but it's still early. Cronometer has some integration capability, and Carb Manager is moving in that direction. For now, most people with a CGM are manually logging notable readings or using the CGM's native app alongside their food tracker separately.

#### Which app is best if I'm also doing intermittent fasting with keto?

Carb Manager has one of the better built-in fasting timers, and it's designed to work alongside macro tracking rather than as a separate feature. Zero is a dedicated fasting app that some people use alongside a separate food tracker if they want more detailed fasting analytics. Either approach works; it just depends on whether you want everything in one place.
