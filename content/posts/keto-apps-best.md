Most people pick a keto app the same way they pick a Netflix show: scroll for two minutes, grab something popular, and wonder why it's not working for them three weeks later. I did exactly that when I first started tracking macros, and I spent an embarrassing amount of time logging food in an app that wasn't even designed with low-carb eating in mind.

Here's what I've learned after years of recommending tracking tools to clients: there's no single "best keto app," and most roundups pretend there is. The app that keeps a busy nurse on track looks nothing like what works for a retired teacher with time to cook elaborate meals. What I can do is tell you what I actually found when I went deep on this, what the apps get right, where they quietly fail, and which ones are genuinely worth your money.

## What Most Keto Apps Get Wrong From the Start

Carb tracking sounds simple. Net carbs, total carbs, fiber math. But most general nutrition apps, MyFitnessPal being the obvious example, were built around calorie-deficit models, not metabolic ones. The database is massive, which is useful, but the default macro targets will push you toward 50% carbohydrates unless you manually override everything. That's not a keto app. That's a calorie counter wearing a keto costume.

What surprised me was how much this matters psychologically. When the app's progress rings and charts are built around calories rather than macro ratios, people default to thinking in calories. They stop asking "will this kick me out of ketosis?" and start asking "does this fit my calorie budget?" Those are genuinely different questions, and the framing shifts everything.

The better keto-specific apps organize your dashboard around fat, protein, and net carbs as the primary metrics. Calories exist, but they're secondary. That single design choice changes how you think about food.

## The Apps Worth Knowing About

**Carb Manager** is what I recommend most often to new clients, and not just because it's popular. It's genuinely built for low-carb eating specifically. The free version covers most of what beginners need: net carb tracking, macro breakdowns, a solid barcode scanner, and meal logging. Premium ($40/year or $8/month if you pay monthly) adds deeper analytics, fasting tracking, blood ketone logging, and recipe analysis. Most people don't need premium for the first few months. Start free.

**Cronometer** is what I personally use for clients doing keto for therapeutic reasons, metabolic health, or who care about micronutrients. It's less polished than Carb Manager but tracks what others ignore: selenium, choline, B vitamins, the full mineral panel. If you want to know whether you're getting enough magnesium from food (most people aren't), Cronometer will show you. The Gold version runs about $45/year. Worth it if you're detail-oriented.

**Keto Diet App** from the team behind the ketogenic diet book series is more of a guided experience. Meal plans, recipes built specifically for keto, macro tracking. The interface is clean. The catch: the food database isn't as comprehensive as Carb Manager, and barcode scanning can struggle with less common products. If you want more structure and less flexibility, this fits.

**Lose It!** comes up constantly in keto communities, and I'll be direct: it's a decent calorie tracker you can sorta bend into a keto tracker. It doesn't belong on a keto-specific list.

About MyFitnessPal specifically: the free version lost offline access and key features in 2022. Premium is now $20/month or $80/year. For keto tracking, that price doesn't make sense when Carb Manager does it better for less.

## The Features That Actually Matter

Database size matters more than people admit. You'll eventually scan a product and get zero results, or three results with wildly different carb counts and no way to know which is right. Carb Manager and MyFitnessPal have the largest databases. Smaller apps can frustrate you if you eat anything outside mainstream groceries.

Net carb vs. total carb calculation is huge. Some apps calculate net carbs automatically (total carbs minus fiber, sometimes sugar alcohols). Others show only total carbs and make you do the math yourself. This sounds minor until you're logging 10 foods a day and everything starts to blur.

Recipe builders deserve more credit. If you cook at home, you need an app that lets you enter ingredients, servings, and gets you per-serving macros. Carb Manager and Cronometer both do this well. This feature alone can prevent the systematic underestimating that stalls most people.

Blood glucose and ketone logging: only Cronometer and Carb Manager handle this meaningfully. If you're using a continuous glucose monitor or testing ketones with a [Keto-Mojo meter](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=keto+mojo+meter&tag=contentportfo-20), logging those readings alongside food data actually matters. Most apps ignore it.

What doesn't matter as much as marketing suggests: social features, step integrations, "AI meal suggestions." They look impressive in screenshots. They rarely change whether someone tracks consistently.

## The Free vs. Paid Question

I get asked this constantly. Start free, pay only when you hit a specific wall.

Carb Manager's free tier handles basic macro tracking, recipe logging, and barcode scanning. That's 80% of what most people need in the first few months. Upgrade if you want detailed nutrient reporting, unlimited meal plans, or ketone logging. Don't pay $8-10/month because animations look nicer.

If you're going to spend money, pair it with things that actually move the needle: a decent [kitchen scale](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=food+scale+kitchen&tag=contentportfo-20) (the OXO Good Grips 11-pound scale is around $50 and worth it), some quality [electrolyte supplements](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=keto+electrolyte+supplements&tag=contentportfo-20) for the first few weeks, and possibly [MCT oil](https://www.amazon.com/s?k=mct+oil+keto&tag=contentportfo-20) if you're using it therapeutically. ## When No App Is the Right App

I want to say this because keto content usually won't: some people do worse with tracking apps. I've had clients with a history of disordered eating for whom obsessive logging made things measurably worse. I've had others who are analytical enough that imperfect data (restaurant meals, guessed portions) created more anxiety than tracking resolved.

The research on self-monitoring generally supports it for dietary adherence, but it's not universal. If you've tried tracking before and found it stressful rather than clarifying, that's real information about yourself. Some people do better with a simpler rule-based approach (no grains, no sugar, keep carbs under 50g, don't count to the gram) than with daily app logging. Talk to a registered dietitian who knows low-carb eating if you're unsure what works for your history.

#### Is Carb Manager actually free, or is it free-to-start?

The core features, including macro tracking, barcode scanning, and meal logging, are genuinely free with no time limit. Some premium features (advanced analytics, unlimited recipes, ketone logging) sit behind the paid tier, but you won't hit a paywall on basic daily tracking.

#### Can I use MyFitnessPal for keto?

You can, but you'll need to manually set your macro targets (typically around 70-75% fat, 20-25% protein, 5-10% carbs) and ignore the app's default guidance. The database is large and useful. The keto-specific experience is clunky compared to apps built for it, and the current premium pricing is hard to justify for low-carb users specifically.

#### How accurate are the food databases in these apps?

Honestly, accuracy varies. Barcode-scanned packaged foods are usually reliable. Restaurant items and user-submitted entries can be significantly off. For anything you eat regularly and care about, cross-check with the USDA FoodData Central database, which is free and reasonably authoritative.

#### Do any keto apps support continuous glucose monitor data?

A few apps are building CGM integrations, but it's still early. Cronometer has some integration capability, and Carb Manager is moving in that direction. For now, most people with a CGM manually log notable readings or use the CGM's native app alongside their food tracker separately.

#### Which app is best if I'm also doing intermittent fasting with keto?

Carb Manager has one of the better built-in fasting timers, and it works alongside macro tracking rather than as a separate feature. Zero is a dedicated fasting app that some people use alongside a separate food tracker if they want more detailed fasting analytics. Either approach works. It's just whether you want everything in one place.
