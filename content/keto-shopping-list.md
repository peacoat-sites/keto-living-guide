Walking into a grocery store without a plan is the fastest way to end up with a cart full of high-carb impulse buys. This checklist solves that. It covers every category you need for a well-stocked keto kitchen, with notes on what to watch for on labels.

Bookmark this page and open it on your phone at the store, or hit **Print List** to take a paper copy. The checklist saves your progress automatically, so if you get interrupted you can pick up where you left off.

## Shopping Notes by Category

**Proteins:** Prioritize fatty cuts over lean ones. Ground beef at 80/20 fat ratio keeps you in the macro range without needing to add as much oil. Chicken thighs beat chicken breasts on keto for the same reason. Always check deli meat labels since many have added dextrose or honey.

**Dairy and fats:** Full-fat everything. Low-fat versions compensate for removed fat with sugar or starch. Heavy cream has less than 1g carbs per tablespoon. MCT oil is optional but useful if you want a quick-absorbing fat for morning coffee or pre-workout.

**Vegetables:** Shop the perimeter. Fresh or frozen both work. Frozen spinach and broccoli are just as nutritious as fresh, and they're cheaper and last longer. Avoid vegetable blends that include corn, peas, or edamame.

**Pantry staples:** Almond flour and coconut flour replace all-purpose flour in keto baking. Psyllium husk adds fiber and improves texture in bread recipes. Bone broth is useful for electrolytes and as a cooking base.

**Condiments:** Read every label. Many "healthy" condiments hide sugar under names like dextrose, maltodextrin, honey, or cane syrup. Mustard and hot sauce are usually safe but double-check. Ranch dressing varies wildly by brand.

## Budget Tips

Keto can get expensive if you lean on specialty products. The cheapest, most reliable keto shopping strategy is to center your cart on eggs, ground beef, cabbage, and canned fish. These four ingredients alone can carry a keto diet for very little per day. Nuts and specialty flours are nice to have but not required when you're starting out.

Buying in bulk helps, especially for proteins. A 5-pound tube of ground beef from a warehouse store costs significantly less per pound than individual packages. Freeze in 1-pound portions and thaw as needed.
