The most important skill on a ketogenic diet is learning to identify net carbs quickly and accurately. Unlike total carbohydrates, net carbs are what actually affect blood sugar and insulin response. Knowing which foods keep you in ketosis and which will kick you out is the foundation of every successful keto meal plan.

Net carbs are calculated by subtracting fiber and sugar alcohols from total carbohydrates. Fiber passes through the digestive system without being absorbed, so it does not raise blood sugar. Sugar alcohols like erythritol and xylitol have minimal glycemic impact and are generally subtracted as well (though some people subtract only half of certain sugar alcohols like maltitol, which has a partial glycemic effect).

The formula: Net Carbs = Total Carbohydrates - Dietary Fiber - Sugar Alcohols

On a standard ketogenic diet, most people aim for 20-50g of net carbs per day to maintain ketosis. Stricter therapeutic keto protocols used for epilepsy management go as low as 10-15g daily. Many people who have been keto-adapted for a long time find they can maintain ketosis at the higher end of that range, while beginners often benefit from staying under 20g until they have established metabolic flexibility.

## Best Keto Foods to Prioritize

The foods at the top of any keto list are those that provide high nutritional value with near-zero net carbs. All plain meats and fish are zero net carbs - beef, chicken, pork, salmon, tuna, shrimp, and lamb are all keto staples. Eggs are another cornerstone food: 2 large eggs have just 1g of net carbs with 13g of protein and 10g of fat.

Fats and oils are completely carb-free. Butter, olive oil, coconut oil, and avocado oil have zero net carbs and are critical for hitting fat targets on keto. Heavy cream (1g per 2 tablespoons) and most hard cheeses (0-1g per ounce) are also excellent.

For vegetables, leafy greens are the gold standard. Spinach, arugula, kale, and romaine lettuce are all under 1g of net carbs per cup. Zucchini, cauliflower, broccoli, and asparagus all come in under 4-5g per half-cup serving, making them reliable staples for filling out meals.

Among nuts, macadamia nuts have the lowest net carbs at just 1g per ounce, followed by pecans (1g), almonds (2g), and walnuts (2g). Cashews are the outlier in the nut world at 8g per ounce - a small handful can use a significant portion of your daily carb budget.

## Foods to Limit or Avoid

Grains are the most problematic category for keto. A single cup of white rice has 45g of net carbs - more than two days' worth on a strict keto protocol. Bread, pasta, oatmeal, and most cereals are similarly high. These are not occasional treats on keto; they are fundamentally incompatible with maintaining ketosis.

Most fruits are too high in sugar for daily keto eating. Bananas (24g), apples (21g), and oranges (15g) are off the table. Blueberries (18g per cup) are borderline. The exception is berries: strawberries, raspberries, and blackberries all come in at 6-7g per cup, making small portions workable for most people.

Legumes present a nuance. They are often positioned as healthy foods, and they are nutritious, but a half-cup of chickpeas or kidney beans has 20-22g of net carbs - not compatible with strict keto, though some people include small amounts on more relaxed low-carb approaches.

Understanding the carb content of what you eat is the most powerful tool you have for making keto work long-term. Use the navigator above to check foods before you buy and build a personal list of your go-to keto staples.
